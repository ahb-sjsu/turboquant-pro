# Your Recall Number Is Hiding Something

TurboQuant Pro 1.9.x is out, and it's the release line where a compression library grew up into a retrieval platform — and where I had to admit that my own favorite metric was lying to me. Again.

Some backstory. This project runs on one rule: compress a tensor for what its consumer does with it, and accept on the consumer's metric — never reconstruction cosine. We learned that rule the hard way when a KV-cache quantizer held cosine ≈ 0.995 while the attention rankings it fed quietly collapsed. Every command in the `tqp` CLI enforces it: rank certificates, consumer-metric probes, gated claim replays.

The 1.9 line applies that rule at scale, and then turns it on a metric I trusted more than cosine: recall itself.

𝗪𝗵𝗮𝘁 𝘀𝗵𝗶𝗽𝗽𝗲𝗱 𝗶𝗻 𝟭.𝟵.𝟬 — search that doesn't fit in RAM. Memory-mapped, sharded, block-streamed indexes; a bit-packed on-disk format (~1.7× smaller); resumable builds that survived a 100-billion-row fleet campaign on preemptible nodes. Rankings stay bit-identical across the format change — compression you can certify, not just admire.

𝗪𝗵𝗮𝘁 𝘀𝗵𝗶𝗽𝘀 𝗶𝗻 𝟭.𝟵.𝟭 — three things:

1️⃣ A query language. `SELECT id, score FROM 'x.tqe' ORDER BY COSINE(:q) LIMIT 10 WITH (RECALL >= 0.95)` — you declare the target, ANALYZE builds a measured calibration catalog, and the planner picks an operating point that meets it. The acceptance rule, expressed as SQL.

2️⃣ An agent surface. JSON-in/JSON-out tools (LangChain, DSPy, MCP, custom GPTs) where the goal is a runtime argument — an agent declares "recall@10 ≥ 0.99" per task and gets back a decision plus a distribution-free certificate.

3️⃣ The one I care most about: 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗮𝗻𝘁𝗶-𝗵𝘂𝗯 𝗼𝗿𝗮𝗰𝗹𝗲.

Here's the uncomfortable part. In high-dimensional embedding spaces, nearest-neighbor relationships aren't symmetric: a few "hub" vectors appear in hundreds of top-10 lists while a large fraction — anti-hubs — appear in almost none. Queries whose true answers are anti-hubs are your hardest retrieval cases, and quantization damages them first, because rounding away fine distinctions is what quantization is.

And aggregate recall barely moves when it happens. The tail collapses; the mean stays green. It's the cosine story all over again, one level up: a blind average hiding the consumer's failure mode.

So 1.9.1 ships instruments instead of averages. `tqp anatomy` tells you what your hubs actually are (density-driven tail, or pipeline-artifact super-hubs — same skewness number, opposite behavior; the scalar is genuinely non-identifying, something we found by measuring both in the wild). `tqp hubdiff` compares exact vs. compressed search — or any two ANN systems — and reports hub-rank agreement, 5th-percentile per-query recall, and anti-hub recall, with a CI gate (`--min-anti-recall`) that fails the build when the tail collapses. In our tests it flags corpora where recall@10 reads a comfortable 0.98 while anti-hub queries sit below 0.30.

If hubness is new to you, I wrote a ten-minute primer with no prerequisites — docs/HUBNESS_PRIMER.md in the repo. It might change how you read your own recall dashboards.

𝗨𝗣𝗗𝗔𝗧𝗘 — 𝘄𝗲 𝗿𝗮𝗻 𝗶𝘁 𝗼𝗻 𝗮 𝗿𝗲𝗮𝗹 𝗺𝘂𝗹𝘁𝗶𝗹𝗶𝗻𝗴𝘂𝗮𝗹 𝗰𝗼𝗿𝗽𝘂𝘀, 𝗮𝗻𝗱 𝗺𝘆 𝗽𝗿𝗲𝗱𝗶𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝘄𝗮𝘀 𝘄𝗿𝗼𝗻𝗴

The tools above now have a stratified mode (we call it STRATA): instead of one verdict for the whole index, every measurement and every gate runs per *area* — in this case, per language — and the verdict is the minimum over areas, not the average. A stratum without enough data doesn't pass quietly; it ABSTAINS, on the record.

We pre-registered predictions, froze them, then measured a 350,000-row sample of a 2.4-million-vector, 40-language corpus (BGE-M3 embeddings). Three findings, including the one that embarrasses me:

1️⃣ 𝗜 𝗽𝗿𝗲𝗱𝗶𝗰𝘁𝗲𝗱 𝗽𝗲𝗿-𝗹𝗮𝗻𝗴𝘂𝗮𝗴𝗲 𝗵𝘂𝗯𝗻𝗲𝘀𝘀 𝘄𝗼𝘂𝗹𝗱 𝘃𝗮𝗿𝘆 𝘄𝗶𝗱𝗲𝗹𝘆. 𝗜𝘁 𝗱𝗼𝗲𝘀𝗻'𝘁. Seven languages spanning 63× in size, three scripts, and about three millennia of text — Hebrew, Aramaic, English, Greek, Latin, Sanskrit, Pali — land in a hubness band (Robin Hood index 0.345–0.447) far narrower than my registered threshold. The encoder homogenizes hubness geometry across languages to a degree nobody at this desk predicted. Scored as NOT CONFIRMED, published anyway: that's what pre-registration is for.

2️⃣ 𝗖𝗼𝗺𝗽𝗿𝗲𝘀𝘀𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗱𝗮𝗺𝗮𝗴𝗲 𝗼𝗿𝗱𝗲𝗿𝘀 𝗯𝘆 𝗿𝗲𝘀𝗼𝘂𝗿𝗰𝗲 𝗹𝗲𝘃𝗲𝗹. At our deployed 27.7× operating point, aggregate recall@10 reads 0.82 — one number, looks fine. Per language, anti-hub recall runs from 0.81 (English) down to 0.74 (Sanskrit), and the three worst strata are exactly the three lowest-resource languages. The effect is real but about half the size I registered, so it's scored below threshold — directionally right is not the same as confirmed, and the scoreboard says so.

3️⃣ 𝗘𝘃𝗲𝗿𝘆 𝗹𝗮𝗻𝗴𝘂𝗮𝗴𝗲 𝗶𝘀 𝗶𝘁𝘀 𝗼𝘄𝗻 𝗵𝘂𝗯 𝗲𝗰𝗼𝗻𝗼𝗺𝘆. All seven measurable strata classify as "local hubs, no global transit." Where cross-language neighbor traffic *does* exist, it's concentrated: Hebrew↔Aramaic (mixed-source corpora) show an order of magnitude more transit than Sanskrit or Pali, which are hermetically self-contained. Retrieval consequences follow directly: a global operating point is being set by your biggest language and silently applied to your smallest.

4️⃣ 𝗔𝗻𝗱 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝘀𝗲𝗰𝗼𝗻𝗱 𝗽𝗿𝗲𝗱𝗶𝗰𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗳𝗲𝗹𝗹 𝘁𝗼𝗼 — 𝗶𝗻𝘃𝗲𝗿𝘁𝗲𝗱. We pre-registered that a translation-trained encoder (LaBSE) would concentrate cross-lingual traffic on a central "translationese" backbone more than an emergent multilingual encoder (BGE-M3). We re-encoded 150,067 identical passages under both models (a paired corpus with per-row text fingerprints, so the comparison is refused unless the texts provably match) and measured the opposite. LaBSE carries MORE cross-lingual neighbor traffic — spread more broadly, through rows *less* central than average, turning 7 of 13 measurable languages into full transit-backbone areas. BGE-M3 keeps language borders intact and routes its smaller cross-lingual flow through a compact central region. Training an interlingua doesn't build a core; it dissolves the borders. Emergent multilinguality keeps the borders and builds a hub. Same texts, two encoders, two architectures of cross-linguality — and a real consequence for RAG: which failure modes you get (border erosion vs. hub concentration) depends on your embedding model's training objective, not your index.

That's two out of two registered mechanism predictions falsified by our own instruments in one day. I can't think of a better advertisement for the instruments — or a worse one for my intuitions.

One more thing worth knowing: hubness stopped being only a quality concern this year. Two 2026 papers (arXiv:2604.05480, arXiv:2602.22427) weaponize and detect it — injected super-hubs near centroids can capture most top-10 lists. The same anatomy report that classifies your hubs doubles as a monitoring instrument: a sudden mechanism shift toward centrality, without a pipeline change, is a poisoning signature.
